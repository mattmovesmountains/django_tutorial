Hot Pi!

How I cooled an overheating(?) Raspberry Pi 4, 8GB

First, the details:

  • Raspberry Pi 4, 8GB model;
  • Four heatsinks previously installed;
  • Use(s):
    • Primary personal computer for basic web/email;
    • Primary computer for coding/web-dev;
    • No graphics/video-heavy applications;
  • "Alright, cut the bullshit, how did you fix it?" Okay, okay...

Step 1: Google Stuff!

Gotta start somewhere; what's the normal temperature for a pi? How do I check mine? Do all pi's get this hot? Did I do something terrible? Should I check WebMD?? No. Never check WebMD.

why is my raspberry pi 4 so fu...

So the problem at-hand: I noticed a little panicky-looking thermometer icon that started blinking at the top right of my screen. When I touched the top of the pi case, it was toasty. But how hot was it normally? It's like smelling the milk... you never smell milk unless you think it's bad, so what's regular milk even smell like??

One good thing about Raspberry Pi is that there is lots of information available to help troubleshoot. The downside is it's often written by the Linux All-Star Team, and the solutions they suggest (whether good or bad) are more puzzling than the original problem that you were trying to solve. And then they try to out-Linux each other in the comment threads. It's often entertaining, but for many, it's not helpful.

After a handful of sites, I was able to gather that 'normal' operating temp is around 45Â°C. I also saw that if you're running temps in the high 70's or cracking the 80's, it's not immediately harmful, but you need to chill out. This is not sustainable.

My pi was frequently reaching high 70's, occasionally breaking the 80 barrier as well, and I was running my codes, but nothing crazy. Sometimes, it was just regular internet usage - not even videos, just text/image sites. Heatsinks installed, AC on in the house... I dunno man, it's just a hot pi. Some of the comments suggested a pi that runs that hot has something else wrong, but I'm not going down that rabbit hole just yet. We all have something wrong - I'm not here to judge.

Oh yeah, do you guys know how to check the temp? It's this command:

vcgencmd measure_temp

Since that's a bit tough on the ol' memory, I created an alias howhot.

Step 2: Buy Stuff!

Whether or not the Raspberry Pi zealots were right about an underlying problem, I decided to solve my problem the old-fashioned American way, by buying stuff! It always seemed pretty clear to me that I needed a better case because even with heatsinks, that stock case has no vents! Where's the shit supposed to go?

So the obvious first choice is to start looking at cases with fans. I recall from a previous Raspberry Pi I built for my nephews as a retropie, that the fan was so damn loud, it was like a little drone. So I researched quiet fans. I'm sure there's plenty of data about which ones are the quietest/loudest, but one piece of information I was missing when I made the retropie was that you can set up a lot of the fans on the 3.3V pin instead of the 5V one. It runs slower, but is considerably quieter.

Armed with that tidbit, it was simply a matter of choosing from a zillion different available options. So I picked the one that was like 8 bucks and has a cool little LED in the fan.

This thing is so quiet. I can't hear it at all, actually.

"BUT!", you say... "does it cool the pi?"
Yes indeed. My average temps are in the 40's now - just like the Pi People told me things should be!

Putting it together was super easy. The fan is plug-n-play. No clearance issues between the new fan and my existing heatsinks. For whatever reason, mine has a white top instead of the clear top that's in the listing, but I haven't the energy to care about such things. If you want this case, you can get it here.
